The 13-bit rule

The full Cosmic Grid cell is 13 bits: one self-bit plus twelve direction bits (A–F and their opposites). That's 8192 = 2 × 4096 — self × directions.

Reading the colours

self-bit · rest matter (v = 0) one direction · photon (v = c) composite radiation mass + radiation

The full-signal view

The rhombic dodecahedron is the FCC cell's Voronoi shell — its 12 faces point at the 12 neighbours exactly. So the 13-bit state draws literally: the self-bit is the translucent body, and every active direction bit is an arrow from the centre to the face it leaves through next tick. Toggle signals for this view, or off for solid voxels.

Evolution = R ∘ T

Transport T: each direction bit hops to its neighbour, keeping its index; the self-bit stays put — that's why it has zero velocity, the discrete meaning of rest mass. Routing R: a single lookup table is applied per cell. Speed is graph distance per tick, so v ≤ c always.

The specimens

rest matter — a lone self-bit, frozen forever (v = 0).
photon — a lone direction bit, streaming straight at v = c.
mass + radiation — self ⊕ direction: the photon flies off, the mass remains.
light-speed gun — the single bijective rule 1→3→6→1 (A → A·B → B·C → A) from one A signal grows a beam at exactly one signal per tick (slope 1), travelling at c. This is the rule proven Turing-complete on FCC.
Glider I / II — moving structures in the broadcast (B/S) projection, lit from asymmetric seeds.

Why self-bit unlocks matter

Without it, every signal must move; the lattice is pure radiation. The self-bit is the one degree of freedom that can stand still, so the same grid carries both massless and massive things — the door to a particle spectrum.
